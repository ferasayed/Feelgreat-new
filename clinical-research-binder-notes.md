## Detailed findings from pages 26-30: Cleveland Clinic publication section

Pages 26-30 expand the **Cleveland Clinic Foundation** publication titled **“Fiber-Multivitamin Combination Therapy: A Beneficial Influence on Low-Density Lipoprotein and Homocysteine”** by **Dennis L. Sprecher** and **Gregory L. Pearce**, published in **Metabolism, Vol. 51, No. 9 (September 2002), pages 1166-1170**. These pages provide the clearest methodology and discussion section in the binder and materially strengthen the credibility of a publishable research summary.

The paper describes a **preliminary double-blind randomized clinical trial** that examined a **high-soluble-fiber mixture supplemented with a vitamin blend**. The text explains that subjects were adults with elevated LDL-C and no major fasting glucose abnormalities. It reports that **99 subjects completed the blinded phase**, split roughly between treatment and placebo. The intervention appears to have used a fiber blend plus vitamins, with dosage details described across the methods section.

### Key reported findings from the article section
The article states that the treatment group experienced a **7.9% reduction in LDL-C from baseline during the 4-week intervention period**, while the placebo group did not show the same benefit. It also notes that compared with placebo the intervention produced an approximately **11.6% lower LDL-C response** at the key endpoint. In the narrative discussion, the authors compare this effect with previously reported fiber-based interventions and suggest the combination may offer useful adjunct support for lipid management.

The publication also reports a reduction in **homocysteine**, which is highlighted as a distinct advantage of including the vitamin component. The binder explains that the formula reduced LDL-C without adverse reduction in HDL-C or major triglyceride penalties often seen with some bile-acid-sequestering approaches. The paper’s discussion further notes an improvement in the **LDL:HDL ratio** and a decrease in **ApoB**, supporting a broader cardiometabolic interpretation rather than a single-marker claim.

### Important methodological details captured
The paper explicitly frames itself as **double-blind** and **randomized**, which makes it one of the stronger items in the binder for scientific presentation on the website. It also includes demographic and dietary intake tables, indicating the authors attempted to account for diet-related confounding. The discussion notes that total caloric intake, total fiber, and carbohydrates were reviewed, while soluble fiber intake changed significantly in the treatment period, supporting the idea that the intervention effect was not simply a reporting artifact.

### Best use in site content
This article should be one of the primary foundations for a research entry about **Balance/Bios Life and LDL/homocysteine support**, because it combines a named journal, identifiable authors, placebo-controlled design, and multiple relevant biomarkers.

### Study A: First-line treatment option in mild hypercholesterolemia
The section titled **“A new fiber drink provides a natural first line treatment option in mild hypercholesterolemia”** is authored by **Vincent Duenas, Julie Duenas, Evelyn Burke, and Peter J. Verdegem**. It was presented at the **7th Annual Conference on Arteriosclerosis, Thrombosis and Vascular Biology** in **Denver, Colorado, April 27-29, 2006**.

The study involved **24 subjects** with **mild hypercholesterolemia** and **LDL-c levels >130 mg/dL**. Participants added the fiber drink to their diet and took it **at least twice daily 15-20 minutes before meals** for **6 weeks**. The drink is described as containing **soluble fiber, minerals, vitamins, policosanol, phytosterols, and Chrysanthemum morifolium extract**.

Reported results include:
- **Total cholesterol (TC >200):** from **229 to 203**, **-11.0%**, **p < 0.000001** in responders
- **LDL-c (>130):** from **157 to 131**, **-16.1%**, **p < 0.001**
- **LDL-c responders (>130):** from **155 to 123**, **-20.4%**, **p < 0.000001**
- **LDL-c (>160):** from **180 to 150**, **-16.6%**, **p < 0.05**
- **LDL-c responders (>160):** from **175 to 140**, **-20.2%**, **p < 0.05**
- **HDL-c (<40):** from **35 to 45**, **+27.9%**, **p < 0.01**
- **HDL-c responders (<40):** from **35 to 47**, **+34.7%**, **p < 0.01**
- **Risk ratio:** from **5.1 to 4.3**, **-15.0%**, **p < 0.0001**
- **Risk ratio responders:** from **5.3 to 4.2**, **-20.8%**, **p < 0.000001**

The discussion says Bios Life Complete combines ingredients known to lower cholesterol through **four different mechanisms**, is very effective in lowering **LDL-c**, increases **HDL-c**, and changed the **TC:HDL ratio** dramatically. The conclusion states that Bios Life Complete offers an effective alternative for patients with mild hypercholesterolemia who cannot or do not want to take statin medication.

### Study B: Adjunct therapy to statin medication in mild hypercholesterolemia
The following section is titled **“A new fiber drink can serve as an adjunct therapy to statin medication in mild hypercholesterolemia”**, by the same authors and conference source.

This study included **11 subjects** with **mild hypercholesterolemia** and **LDL-c >130 mg/dL** who were already on statin therapy but added the drink daily **15-20 minutes before meals**. The drink again contained **soluble fiber, minerals, vitamins, policosanol, phytosterols, and Chrysanthemum morifolium extract**.

- **Total cholesterol (responders):** from **228 to 199**, **-12.8%**, **p < 0.001**

- **LDL-c responders:** from **151 to 108**, **-28.4%**, **p < 0.001**
- **HDL-c:** from **34 to 42**, **+23.2%**, **p < 0.05**
- **HDL-c responders:** from **34 to 43**, **+26.3%**, **p < 0.05**
- **Risk ratio:** from **5.2 to 4.4**, **-15.2%**, **p < 0.01**
- **Risk ratio responders:** from **5.4 to 4.3**, **-18.9%**, **p < 0.001**

The discussion notes that statin users whose lipids had not changed significantly over 6 months showed an additional drop in LDL-c after adding the fiber drink, while HDL-c also increased. The binder concludes that Bios Life Complete is a promising adjunct option for patients with hypercholesterolemia already using statins, especially when statin effects have plateaued.

- **Study 1:** LDL-c **168 to 127 (-24.5%, p < 0.0001)** and HDL-c **32 to 37 (+12.0%, n.s.)**
- **Study 2 (adjunct to statins):** LDL-c **150 to 118 (-21.1%, p < 0.05)** and HDL-c **34 to 42 (+23.2%, p < 0.05)**
- **Study 3:** LDL-c **154 to 119 (-22.5%, p < 0.05)** and HDL-c **25 to 30 (+20.2%, n.s.)**

The conclusion explicitly says the intervention lowers cholesterol through **four different mechanisms**, is effective in lowering LDL-c and increasing HDL-c, and may serve both as an alternative for mild hypercholesterolemia and in combination with statin medication.

### Study 25: Lipid and glucose optimization using phytonutrient combination therapy in diabetes

### Study 26: Bios Life Slim and glycemic index of starchy foods
Pages 77-79 finally provide a direct **Bios Life Slim** study. The title is **“The effects of a fiber-rich nutritional supplement, Bios Life Slim, on the glycemic index of three starchy foods”** by **Jennie Brand-Miller, Fiona Atkinson, and Thomas Cutler**.

The background states that glycemia is an accurate indicator for overall health and long-term high-glycemic diets are associated with diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and obesity. The **objective** was to quantify the impact of a fiber-rich supplement on the **glycemic index (GI)** of **white bread, white rice, and instant mashed potatoes**.

The **design** involved **10 lean healthy subjects** who consumed **50 grams of glucose in water** and three starchy foods along with **7.25 g of Bios Life Slim** or **14.5 g of Bios Life Slim**. Standardized methods were used, with plasma glucose and insulin sampled for **2 hours** and areas under the curve calculated.

The reported **results** are especially clear:

- **One dose** of Bios Life Slim reduced the glycemic index of **white rice by 16.0%**, **instant mashed potatoes by 16.9%**, and **white bread by 20.5%**.
- **Two doses** of Bios Life Slim reduced the glycemic index of **instant mashed potatoes by 20.4%**, **white rice by 24.7%**, and **white bread by 27.7%**.
- **One dose** reduced the **insulinemic index** of instant mashed potatoes, white rice, and white bread by **10.4%**, **16.9%**, and **21.3%** respectively.
- **Two doses** reduced the insulinemic index of instant mashed potatoes, white bread, and white rice by **15.1%**, **27.0%**, and **27.3%** respectively.
